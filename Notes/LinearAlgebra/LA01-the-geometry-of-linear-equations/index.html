LA01: The geometry of linear equations

The fundamental problem of linear algebra is to solve n linear equations in n unknowns, for example:

2x−y = 0−x+2y = 3\begin{aligned} 2x-y\,=\,0\\ -x+2y\,=\,3 \end{aligned}2x−y=0−x+2y=3​

In the first lecture, Dr. Strang show us three ways to view this problem.

Row Picture

Plot the points that satisfied each equation. The intersection of the plots (if they do intersect) is the solution of the system of above equations, which is x = 1,y = 2x\,=\,1, y\,=\,2x=1,y=2.

Figure1: The plots of the above equations intersect at the point (1,2)

We substitute (1,2) into the original system of equations to check it’s validity:

2∗1−2 = 0−1∗+2∗2 = 3\begin{aligned}2*1-2\,=\,0\\-1*+2*2\,=\,3\end{aligned} 2∗1−2=0−1∗+2∗2=3​

Similarly, the solution of a three dimensional system is the common intersection of those three planes (if there does exist one).
